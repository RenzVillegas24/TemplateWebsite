    The Internet Of Things

    The "Internet of Things" (IoT) is becoming a more popular topic of discussion both in and out of the workplace. It's an idea with the potential to change not only how we live but also how we work. But what precisely is the "Internet of Things," and how will it affect you, if at all? There are many intricacies around the "Internet of Things," but I'd want to focus on the fundamentals. Many technical and policy-related discussions are taking place, but many individuals are still attempting to grasp the gist of what these discussions are about.

    What is it?

    Simply expressed, this is the idea of connecting any device having an on/off switch to the Internet (and/or to each other) over the Internet. Cellphones, coffee makers, washing machines, headphones, lamps, wearable devices, and almost anything else fall into this category. This also applies to machine components, such as an airplane's jet engine or an oil rig's drill.

    According to Gartner, there will be over 26 billion linked devices by 2020... There are a lot of links there (some even estimate this number to be much higher, over 100 billion).     The Impacts.

    "Anything that can be connected, will be connected," will be the new rule for the future. But why would you want so many connected gadgets to communicate with one another? There are numerous instances of what this could look like or how valuable it could be. Let's say you're driving to a meeting; your car may have access to your schedule and already know the optimal path. If there is heavy traffic, your automobile may send a text message to the other party informing them that you will be late. What if your alarm clock wakes you up at 6 a.m. and then sends a signal to your coffee maker to begin brewing coffee for you? What if your workplace equipment detected when supplies were getting short and automatically reordered more? What if your workplace wearable gadget could tell you when and where you were most active and productive, and then share that data with other devices you used at work?

    On a larger scale, the Internet of Things may be used to improve transportation networks and create "smart cities," which can help us minimize waste and increase efficiency in areas like energy use, allowing us to better understand and improve how we work and live. The truth is that the Internet of Things opens up nearly unlimited possibilities and connections, many of which we can't even imagine or completely comprehend today. It's easy to see how and why the Internet of Things is such a popular issue today; it certainly opens up a lot of opportunities, but it also presents a lot of obstacles.

    Security is a major concern that is frequently raised. What can people do to ensure that their data is secure in a world when billions of gadgets are connected? Will a hacker be able to gain access to your entire network by hacking into your toaster? The Internet of Things also exposes businesses all around the world to new security risks. Then there's the problem of data sharing and privacy. Even now, this is a contentious issue, so one can only image how the debate and worries will grow when we're talking about billions of connected gadgets. Another challenge that many businesses will confront is the large volumes of data that all of these gadgets will generate. Companies must devise a method for storing, tracking, analyzing, and interpreting the massive volumes of data that will be generated.

    What about now?

    Conversations concerning the Internet of Things are taking place (and have been for several years) all around the world as we try to figure out how it will affect our lives. We're also trying to figure out what the various opportunities and problems will be when more and more devices join the Internet of Things. For the time being, the greatest thing we can do is educate ourselves on the Internet of Things and its potential impacts on how we work and live.
